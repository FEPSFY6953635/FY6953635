Introduction

       Energy is a necessity for survival and this demand for energy increases day-by-day, as population increases.
       This matched alongside modern society’s dependency on electricity for digital technology and transport as well as essentials like heating.
       The energy systems currently available rely on materials that waste energy through heating, lowering efficiency and increasing costs.
       However, zero-loss materials called superconductors provide absolutely no resistance when carrying an electrical current and could be the key to solving these energy challenges - 
      electrical systems can operate with fewer losses and higher magnetic field strengths nearly indefinitely.
       Despite, the optimism offered by there are hurdles that must be addressed to establish the use of superconductors in future mainstream global energy systems.

Preventing Energy Loss

    Conventional electricity systems lose significant amounts of energy through resistive heating in copper and aluminium cables.
    High-temperature superconducting (HTS) cables can carry current with zero resistance, eliminating these losses.
    They also allow higher current densities and smaller footprints, ideal for dense urban environments.
    Engineering challenges remain, particularly cryogenic cooling systems that must maintain extremely low temperatures safely and efficiently.

Power Transmission: Superconducting Cables for Efficient Grids

    UK distribution networks lose around 7.2% of electricity consumed due to resistive heating.
    HTS cables demonstrated in the US, Korea, and Germany show potential for reducing losses and increasing efficiency.
    Cryogenic system design (liquid helium/nitrogen, insulation, coolant circulation) is complex and costly but essential.

Generating Energy

    Beyond transmission, superconductors can revolutionize energy generation systems.
    Motors, generators, and fusion reactors benefit from stronger magnetic fields and higher efficiency.
    These technologies could reduce costs and improve renewable energy production.

Motors and Generators: Superconductors for High Power Density

    Conventional machines suffer copper and core losses, reducing efficiency.
    Superconducting coils enable stronger magnetic fields, higher torque, and lighter offshore wind generators.
    Challenges include cryogenic cooling complexity, material cost, and mechanical delicacy.

Fusion Energy: Superconductors Enabling Plasma Confinement

    Fusion requires extreme magnetic fields to confine plasma at over 100 million °C.
    HTS magnets allow stronger fields, smaller reactors, and lower costs.
    Challenges: high material cost, radiation resilience, and plasma stability.
    Superconductors are regarded as key enabling technologies for future fusion energy.
